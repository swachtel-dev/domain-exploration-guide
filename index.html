Domain Exploration Guide

FINAN VIII: Future of Finance — Final Project

How to use this guide: Start by reading all six domain summaries below (click each card to expand it). Get the full landscape before committing to one domain. Then dig into the one or two that interest you most. Each domain includes two to three example problems with real data, connections to course units you have already completed, and links to help you start researching.

Choosing one of the 18 example problems listed below is completely fine; many strong proposals start there. You can also narrow a listed problem further, combine elements from different examples, or find a related problem within the same domain. What matters is that your final problem is specific, quantifiable, and addressable by a token mechanism.

Choosing Well

Before you start browsing, keep three things in mind:

  1. The problem is specific and quantified. "Healthcare is broken" is not a problem statement. "80% of clinical trials take 6+ months to recruit participants, adding $1.3M per month in delays" is.
  2. The token mechanism addresses the problem directly. The token should solve the specific incentive misalignment, trust deficit, or coordination failure you identified. If you remove the token from your design and everything still works, it was never needed.
  3. You care about the domain. You will spend five units on this project. Genuine interest in the problem produces better research, sharper analysis, and a more compelling white paper.
Domain 1
Financial Inclusion & Payments
Cross-border transfers, credit access, payment processing
▼
Cross-border remittances

Migrant workers sent $656 billion to low- and middle-income countries in 2022, paying an average fee of 6.2%. Existing solutions (Western Union, bank wires, mobile money, crypto bridges) each face adoption, compliance, or volatility barriers that keep fees stubbornly high.

Thin-file credit access

An estimated 1.4 billion adults globally lack a bank account. In the US alone, 26 million adults are "credit invisible" with no file at the major bureaus. Alternative data approaches (you studied these in Unit 3) exist but remain centralized and non-portable across lenders.

Small business payment costs

Small merchants pay average card processing fees of 2.6%, compressing margins on low-ticket transactions. Real-time settlement and lower-cost rails could redirect billions in economic activity, but switching costs remain high.

Domain 2
Supply Chain & Trade Finance
Provenance tracking, trade documents, agricultural certification
▼
Pharmaceutical authentication

The WHO estimates 1 in 10 medical products in low- and middle-income countries is substandard or falsified. Serialization mandates generate tracking data, but that data is siloed across manufacturers, distributors, and pharmacies with no shared trust layer.

Trade finance accessibility

The global trade finance gap exceeds $2.5 trillion (Asian Development Bank), hitting small and medium-sized enterprises in emerging markets hardest. Documentary credit processes involve 5-10 intermediaries and take 5-10 days to settle.

Agricultural provenance

Fair trade and organic certifications command price premiums, but verification is difficult and fraud is common. Coffee buyers pay premiums that may not reach farmers. Tokenized provenance could create an auditable chain from farm to consumer.

Domain 3
Capital Markets & Asset Tokenization
Fractional ownership, private equity access, illiquid asset markets
▼
Fractional real estate

The median US home price exceeds $400,000. REITs (real estate investment trusts) offer some access but limit individual property selection. Tokenized fractional ownership could lower barriers while preserving investor control over specific assets.

Private equity access

Global private equity exceeds $4.7 trillion in assets under management, but accredited investor requirements lock out the vast majority of the population. Tokenized fund shares with programmable compliance could democratize access while maintaining regulatory guardrails.

Illiquid asset secondary markets

Art, collectibles, private company shares, and similar assets have no efficient secondary market. Holders face years-long lock-ups with no price discovery. Tokenization could create liquid markets for fundamentally illiquid assets.

Domain 4
Digital Identity & Credentials
Know Your Customer (KYC) compliance, credential portability, self-sovereign identity
▼
Know Your Customer (KYC) compliance costs

Financial institutions are required to verify the identity of every customer before opening an account—a process called Know Your Customer, or KYC. Globally, institutions spend an estimated $214 billion annually on this and related anti-money-laundering compliance. Each institution independently verifies the same customer from scratch. A shared, privacy-preserving verification layer could eliminate duplicated work without centralizing sensitive data.

Educational credential portability

Students who transfer institutions, pursue continuing education, or seek employment internationally face months of manual transcript verification. No widely adopted system allows verified credentials to travel across borders and institutions.

Self-sovereign identity for the unbanked

Many of the 1.4 billion unbanked adults worldwide lack government-issued identification, which is a prerequisite for opening any financial account. Self-sovereign identity—where individuals own and control their own credentials instead of depending on a single government or company to issue them—could allow people to build verifiable reputations without a central authority.

Domain 5
Energy & Environmental Markets
Carbon credits, renewable energy certificates, peer-to-peer trading
▼
Carbon credit integrity

The voluntary carbon market reached $2 billion in 2022, but investigations have found that many offset categories may not represent real emission reductions. Tokenized carbon credits with on-chain verification could establish provenance and prevent double-counting.

Renewable energy certificate (REC) trading

Renewable energy certificates, or RECs, are tradable documents that verify electricity was generated from a renewable source. Markets for RECs are fragmented and small generators are often excluded. Tokenized RECs could enable real-time settlement and lower participation barriers for rooftop solar and community installations.

Peer-to-peer energy trading

Households with rooftop solar generate surplus electricity that feeds back to the grid at wholesale rates. Smart-contract-based peer-to-peer trading could capture more value for producers while lowering costs for nearby consumers.

Domain 6
Governance & Community Coordination
Public goods funding, community investment, transparent giving
▼
Public goods funding

Open-source software, academic research, and public infrastructure benefit everyone but are chronically underfunded because contributors cannot capture the value they create. Quadratic funding (pioneered by Gitcoin) demonstrates one token-based approach, but gaming and low adoption remain persistent challenges.

Community investment coordination

Neighborhood-level investment decisions (parks, infrastructure, small business support) are typically made by centralized authorities with limited community input. Token-based governance—sometimes organized as a decentralized autonomous organization, or DAO, where token holders vote directly on proposals—could give residents direct, proportional voice in how community development funds are allocated.

Transparent charitable giving

US charitable giving reached $557 billion in 2023, but donors have limited visibility into how funds are spent downstream. On-chain donation tracking with milestone-based disbursement could increase accountability without requiring a centralized auditor.
